📖 Algorithm Overview
Greedy Paradigm

Problem Statement

Maximize total profit Σ pi by scheduling jobs with deadlines. Each job Ji has profit pi and deadline di, requiring exactly 1 unit of time. Constraint: job must complete by di.

The Greedy Strategy

  • Step 1: Sort by Profit — Arrange jobs in descending order of profit: p₁ ≥ p₂ ≥ ⋯ ≥ pn.
  • Step 2: Place Greedily — For each job, search for the latest possible free slot from t = di down to 1. If found, assign there; otherwise discard.

Why Greedy Works

Greedy Choice Property: Selecting the highest-profit job first does not prevent an optimal solution. Lower-profit jobs never dominate better outcomes.
Exchange Argument: If an optimal solution excludes our high-profit choice, we can swap it in place of a lower-profit job, maintaining optimality via slot rearrangement.

Complexity Summary

  • Time: O(n log n) sort + O(n log n) slot search (via Union-Find) = O(n log n) total.
  • Space: O(n) for schedule array and job list.

Input

Note: Each job must have positive profit (>0). Deadline can be 1–100. Jobs are sorted by profit when enabled.

Educational Content

Overview: What this algorithm does

Job Scheduling with Deadlines selects a subset of jobs to maximize total profit. Each job takes one unit time and must finish before its deadline. The greedy strategy sorts jobs by profit (high to low), then places each job into the latest free slot before its deadline.

Paradigm: Greedy. Choice: highest-profit remaining job. Local decision is globally effective here because later-slot placement preserves earlier slots for tighter-deadline jobs.

📝 Static Walkthrough (Step-by-Step)

Input: J1(60, 2), J2(100, 1), J3(20, 3), J4(40, 2)

  1. Sort by profit: J2(100), J1(60), J4(40), J3(20).
  2. Process J2 (d=1): Check slot 1 → free. Schedule in S1. Current: [J2, _, _]
  3. Process J1 (d=2): Check slot 2 → free. Schedule in S2. Current: [J2, J1, _]
  4. Process J4 (d=2): Check slot 2 → occupied (J1). Check slot 1 → occupied (J2). No free slot. Discard J4.
  5. Process J3 (d=3): Check slot 3 → free. Schedule in S3. Final: [J2, J1, J3]

Final Profit: 100 + 60 + 20 = 180.

Note: J4 was discarded despite having decent profit (40) because both available slots were already claimed by higher-profit jobs.

Key Idea: Always place selected job as late as possible. This protects early slots for jobs with tight deadlines.
Common Mistake: Placing jobs in earliest free slot can block future high-profit jobs that have no flexibility.
CLO-2 Connection: Sorting (by profit) and searching (for free slot) are both essential. Remove sorting and total profit usually drops.

CLO-2: Sorting & Searching
Role of Sorting (Line 1): Jobs are sorted by profit descending via comparison sort — O(n log n). This guarantees the highest-value candidates are evaluated first. Without it, a low-profit job can occupy a slot that a high-profit job needs.
Role of Searching (Lines 4–6): For each job, searching for a free slot uses Union-Find with path compression — O(log n) amortized per job, O(n log n) total. Searching latest-first is critical: it preserves earlier slots for future tighter-deadline jobs.

CLO-3: Greedy Paradigm
Paradigm: Greedy. At each step, pick the highest-profit remaining job and assign it to the latest available slot before its deadline. No backtracking. One pass through the sorted list.
Why it works — Exchange Argument: Suppose an optimal schedule OPT skips job J (high profit) and uses job K (lower profit) in the same slot. Swapping K → J can only increase total profit. By induction, every greedy choice is compatible with some optimal solution.
Counterexample — EDF is Suboptimal: Earliest-Deadline-First schedules GA(profit=5, d=1) first because it has the earliest deadline, wasting slot 1 and blocking GD(profit=80). Greedy bypasses GA entirely and packs GC+GD+GE for profit 240 vs EDF’s 165. Load Preset D to see this comparison.
